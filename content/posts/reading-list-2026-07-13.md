## 1. Elias Torres — *"The $1.2B exit was my biggest failure — because we stopped being obsessed with customers"*

[Elias Torres](https://www.linkedin.com/in/eliast) (founder and CEO of [Agency](https://www.agency.inc), previously co-founder and CTO of Drift) joined GTMnow this week to explain why he calls his $1.2 billion exit the biggest failure of his career. The headline number is wrong, in his framing: once Torres lost control of Drift, the company was sold onward, the team was let go, and the product was eventually shut down. Success isn't the exit multiple — it's whether you build something customers stay obsessed with. Letting down customers and team is the real failure worth learning from.

What he's building at Agency reflects that framing directly. The operating model: fewer than 100 people total, 80-90% of them engineers, and everyone in the company talks to customers. His goal isn't to scale headcount — it's to get 50 customers completely obsessed before expanding. On AI specifically: Torres is explicit that the advantage isn't the precision of every output, it's the aggregate productivity unlocked at scale. He lets Agency draft and send his own email automatically — the gain from reaching ten times as many people outweighs the loss from any individual message being imperfect. Distribution remains the hardest problem in the AI era, he argues, and the only durable foundation for it is genuine customer obsession. Not NPS. Obsession.

**Why it matters for Mallin.** Torres is making the operating-layer case from the customer-success side: the AI stack compounds into *retention*, not just acquisition. For teams building on the Mallin architecture, the question his framing raises is whether the obsession signal is real — whether the 50-customer depth test exists before the scale lever gets pulled. A motion built on churn tolerance doesn't compound; it just accelerates.

→ [GTMnow #197: Why a $1.2B Exit Felt Like His Biggest Failure](https://gtmnow.com/gtm-197-ai-customer-organization-elias-torres-agency/) (July 2, 2026); [Agency.inc](https://www.agency.inc) for context on what he's building

## 2. Mark Roberge — *"Your north star metric is probably wrong — and AI will get you to the wrong destination faster"*

[Mark Roberge](https://www.linkedin.com/in/markroberge) (co-founder, Stage 2 Capital; founding CRO of HubSpot) joined Gainsight's [Un]Churned podcast this week to make the case that most companies are optimizing for the wrong metric. The central argument from his new book *The Science of Scaling*: the decision of when and how fast to scale shouldn't be a gut call — it should be gated on retention. Specifically, he argues you need a Leading Indicator of Retention (LIR): one metric that predicts, within the customer's first month, whether they're going to churn. Revenue is a dangerous north star because it lags the real signal by months. By the time churn shows up in the number, the structural problem has been compounding for a quarter or more.

On what "AI-native" should actually mean in 2026: Roberge is direct that it needs numbers attached, not just language. The metric he returns to is selling time — historically 25-30% of a rep's week, with the rest going to prep, admin, CRM updates. AI-native teams are measurably pushing that toward 75%. But the leap isn't about removing tasks; it's about changing what the role is — more ownership per rep, more direct customer contact, less handoff chain. He draws a sharp distinction from the hype framing: "AI will fix your funnel" is not what this is. "AI gives the reps who already have the right foundation more time to use it" is closer.

**Why it matters for Mallin.** The LIR framing maps directly to the operating layer: Mallin's value is in surfacing the right leading indicators for post-sale customer behavior, not trailing revenue metrics. Roberge is describing the measurement architecture that has to exist before AI surfaces in the rep workflow. Building on a lagging north star and adding AI velocity is how you accelerate in the wrong direction.

→ [Gainsight [Un]Churned: Your North Star Metric Is Wrong (and It's Killing Your Retention)](https://www.gainsight.com/episode/your-north-star-metric-is-wrong-and-its-killing-your-retention-ft-mark-roberge-stage-2-capital/) (July 2026); his book [*The Science of Scaling*](https://www.stage2.capital/science-of-scaling-book) is the long-form version

## 3. Brian Le — *"Trust is the ultimate currency in RevOps — and comp is the first place it breaks"*

[Brian Le](https://www.linkedin.com/in/brianhoangle/) (Global Sales Compensation Partner at Notion) walked through how to build a scalable sales compensation operating system on GTMnow this week. Le scaled Notion's sales comp from 80 to 400+ employees and his central argument is that the most important output of a comp plan isn't the number — it's the trust signal it sends. Reps read comp as a message about what the company actually values. When that signal is misaligned — high quota, low base, vague accelerators — you lose credibility before you lose the rep.

His operational framing is built around detecting early: comp plans degrade before attrition happens, and they degrade in specific ways you can watch for. The structural variables he focuses on are pay mix (the ratio of base to variable), accelerator design, and whether the upside math is actually achievable — not in theory, but for a median rep in a median quarter. One benchmark worth noting: in well-designed plans, top AEs can pull $1M+ in a single quarter. That number is a design signal, not a floor — it tells you the ceiling is built to be real rather than aspirational fiction. The comp operating system is infrastructure. Most teams treat it as incentive design.

**Why it matters for Mallin.** Comp is data. What it rewards, how it's structured, and whether reps believe in it is upstream of every pipeline signal that flows through the revenue system. For any operating-layer architecture, comp design is one of the highest-leverage configuration points — and one of the most neglected in RevOps conversations that default to tooling before incentive alignment. Bad comp design is invisible until it isn't.

→ [GTMnow #198: The Compensation Blueprint for a High-Performing Sales Team](https://gtmnow.com/gtm-198-sales-compensation-plan-blueprint-brian-le-notion/) (July 9, 2026); [Brian Le on scaling comp at Notion](https://www.forma.ai/resources/article/scaling-sales-compensation-with-brian-le) (Forma.ai, additional context)

## 4. Tom Snyder & Zeev Wexler — *"AI doesn't fix bad sales processes — it exposes them"*

[Tom Snyder](https://www.linkedin.com/in/snydertom/) (founder of Funnel Clarity, sales process researcher with decades of data on what actually moves deals) and Zeev Wexler (CEO, Wexler) appeared together on the Sales Game Changers podcast this week with the clearest framing I've heard on the AI-in-sales hype problem. The centerpiece: "AI doesn't fix bad sales processes, it exposes them. If you accelerate a broken workflow, you're not improving performance; you're simply making mistakes happen faster. Build the right sales process first, then let AI amplify it." It's not a new idea — but the way they make it operational is worth sitting with.

The corollary they draw: the default AI implementation mistake is using AI to increase volume on something that never worked at the required volume. Snyder's version: "The goal of AI is not to send one hundred times more emails that failed before. The goal is to send the same number of emails and make them ten times more effective." The distinction between volume amplification and quality amplification is the real fork in the road for AI sales deployment — and most teams default to volume because it's the obvious metric. What's harder to measure, and what Snyder has built his research around, is whether the process itself is sound before you touch the acceleration layer.

**Why it matters for Mallin.** This is the operating-layer argument from the process side. Mallin's architecture surfaces what's happening in the revenue motion — and what Snyder and Wexler are describing is what happens when you add AI velocity to a motion that hasn't been diagnosed. The quality signal has to precede the automation. Otherwise you're scaling noise.

→ [Sales Game Changers Ep. 857: Implementing Sales Workflows That AI Can Actually Improve](https://www.salesgamechangerspodcast.com/aiandsales6/) (July 7, 2026)

---

**Common thread this week.** All four pieces make the same argument from different angles: the operating foundation precedes the AI surface. Torres: obsession has to be real before you scale it. Roberge: your north star has to point at retention, not revenue, before you press the accelerator. Le: comp has to signal trust before it signals output. Snyder and Wexler: your process has to work before AI makes it faster. The pattern holds. AI is a forcing function — it surfaces what's already there. The teams pulling ahead aren't the ones that added AI to a broken system. They're the ones that built the right foundation first, then added AI.
