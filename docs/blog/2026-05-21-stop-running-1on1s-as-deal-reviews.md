# Stop running 1:1s as deal reviews.

*What changes when the recap doesn't have to happen anymore — and what you do with the forty minutes you just got back.*

---

The average sales manager 1:1 is sixty minutes. Forty of those minutes are recap.

"Walk me through Maven Beauty." "Where are we on Hirschi?" "What changed since last week?" The rep narrates; the manager listens, reacts, asks a clarifying question, occasionally pushes on something. Then five minutes of actual coaching, five minutes of "what do you need from me," five minutes of small talk, done.

This was always a bad use of an hour. It happens because the manager wasn't on the call, didn't read the Gong transcript, and the rep is the only source of current truth on the deal. Information transfer eats the meeting.

The reason it persists is that there's been no alternative. Until recently, the rep *was* the only current read on the deal. You couldn't review it without them in the room.

That's changing — and changing fast.

## What the live read now does for itself

Tools that read transcripts, emails, calendar, Slack, and CRM as a unified surface have hit a point where they maintain a current picture of every deal continuously. Not weekly summaries. Continuous. The pre-call brief is current within minutes of a call landing. So is the next brief, after the follow-up. So is the picture of what's at risk, who went quiet, which stakeholder just lateraled in from a competitor's portfolio.

If you walk into your 1:1 with that brief already read — and your rep walks in with the same view — the recap is done before you sit down.

So what happens to the other forty minutes?

## The conversation that's actually worth having

You stop asking "what happened" and start asking "what does it mean that this happened?"

This is the cognitive level managers were supposed to be working at all along. The manager's edge over the rep isn't deal knowledge — the rep has more of that. It's judgment about what to do with the deal knowledge. That judgment doesn't show up in a Gong transcript. It shows up in conversation.

Concretely:

You read the brief before the meeting. You see that Maven Beauty's new FP&A hire came laterally from Lumen Botanicals — another Heritage Brands portfolio brand. You also see your rep didn't ask about Heritage IT involvement on the discovery call. Mallin flags this as a missed thread. You flag it too.

The 1:1 starts there. Not "tell me about Maven Beauty" but: "Cara came from another Heritage brand. You didn't ask whether Heritage IT is in the buying process. What were you thinking in the moment?"

That's a coaching question specific to a moment, not a methodology. The rep tells you they almost asked but felt it would be too direct in a first call. You talk about why "too direct" was the wrong frame. You workshop the exact words for the follow-up.

That conversation — six minutes — is more useful than the forty minutes of recap you used to do.

## Three other things you do with the reclaimed time

**Stakeholder strategy.** "The CEO sits at the bottom of your access ladder on this deal. The Wednesday demo is your shot to surface her. How are we engineering that?" You couldn't ask this question in five minutes at the end of a recap meeting. With recap gone, you have thirty.

**Pattern across deals.** "I'm seeing three of your deals stall when the buyer's COO enters late in the cycle. What do we think is going on?" This question requires reading across the rep's whole pipeline; the brief makes that possible. Without it, the manager could only see one deal at a time, in narrative.

**The rep's growth.** Career conversations actually happen, because there's time for them. The 1:1 becomes a place where the rep's *path* is the topic, not just their pipeline. You'd be surprised how rare that's been for the last decade.

## What you keep doing manually

Not everything in a 1:1 should be augmented. The judgment moments — when to push, when to pause, when the rep is psychologically in a place where they need to vent before they can be coached — those don't show up in any brief. They require you being present.

Don't read the brief in the meeting. Read it before. Use the meeting for the part the brief can't do.

## The new shape of the 1:1

The shape that works for the next year:

The first ten minutes is the rep walking in with one thing — a moment from the week they want to think through. Not a recap. One coaching moment they've already chosen.

The next twenty is your read of the brief — but only the parts where you see something the rep didn't. You're not telling them what happened. You're telling them what you noticed about what happened.

The next twenty is stakeholder and pattern work. Cross-deal questions. The thing that requires both of you in the same room thinking.

The last ten is the rep's career. Not "what do you need from me" as a closing pleasantry. The actual conversation about where they're going.

This is a sixty-minute meeting with no recap section, and it grows people more than the old shape ever did.

## What this means for the management job

If you're a sales manager whose primary value-add was knowing every deal in detail — the agentic shift changes what you're paid for. The deals are knowable without you. What's needed from you is judgment about what to do with what's known.

That's a harder job, not an easier one. But it's the one that grows your team. Recap was never coaching. Now you don't have to pretend it was.
