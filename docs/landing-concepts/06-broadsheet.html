The forecast is the report. The truth is somewhere else.

Pipelines look healthy. Forecast categories say yes. Then the quarter slips on three deals nobody saw collapsing. A new operating layer reads what the CRM cannot.

By The Editors  ·  Filed under: Operating Layers, Revenue, Verification

Every quarter, leadership sits down on a Friday morning and asks the same question about a handful of deals: is this real? The CRM says 75 percent, commit, advancing. The rep says the champion is engaged. The forecast category says yes.

Meanwhile the actual signals — the CFO has not joined a call in six weeks, the champion's airtime collapsed from forty percent to eight, procurement entered late, a competitor was introduced during pricing — sit fragmented across calls, Slack, email, calendar invites, and stakeholder behavior. The data exists. It is not synthesized. It does not reach the forecast call.

"It would have provided a way for us to address it head‑on versus skirting around it."
— From the team that ran the closed‑lost cycle, after replaying it through Mallin

Mallin is built to do one thing well: read every artifact on a deal — calls, emails, Slack threads, calendar invites, CRM activity — and reconstruct the operational state from what is already there. No new logging. No new workflow. The substrate the rep produced by doing the deal is the substrate Mallin runs on.

From that substrate, verification alerts surface before forecast day. Signer disengagement. Champion airtime collapse. Procurement entered late. Competitor introduced during pricing. The agent decides what is a gap; the team decides the response. The manager being in the deal Slack thread means the rep cannot quietly skirt the conversation.

Five enterprise cycles tested in retrospect. Twelve alerts fired on the closed‑lost. Zero false‑positive escalations. Approval gate on every CRM write. Stage, amount, close date, and forecast category never auto‑written — even after trust is earned.

— In this issue

A note on the name.

A mallín is a kind of Patagonian wetland that looks like ordinary grassland from above but is sustained by an underground current flowing beneath it. Pull the grass back and the water is right there. We took the name because it described, with no embellishment, the thing we kept finding inside enterprise sales cycles: the surface gets typed into the CRM; the underground flow is what actually sustains the deal.

— Section II · Mechanism

Three primitives. No chatbot.

Read. Every artifact on the deal — calls, email, Slack, calendar, CRM — becomes substrate. Interpret. Behavior is the signal; activity is the noise. Act. The manager is in the thread before the rep can skirt.

— Section III · Trust contract

Earned reliance, not assumed reliance.

Every CRM field passes through three stages. Suggest shows the read with no write. Apply earns one‑click approve only after confirm‑rate data clears. Auto + Confirm writes first and surfaces an undo — unlocked only after Stage 2 hesitation data clears.

Never auto-written. Stage. Amount. Close date. Forecast category. The cost of getting these wrong is a missed quarter, not a deleted record.
