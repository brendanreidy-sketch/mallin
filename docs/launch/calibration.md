# Calibration — what real signal sounds like

*Pre-write what landing actually sounds like, so polite interest doesn't get mistaken for it. Pull this up before any outreach call. Re-read it after.*

## The trap

In the moment, every response feels meaningful. A nod, a *"yeah,"* a *"send me what you have"* — they all feel like signal. Most aren't. They're the social cost of being polite to someone they know.

The only way to not over-interpret is to pre-commit to what landing actually sounds like, in their words, before you hear them say anything.

## The one question to anchor every call

Ask this **early**, then **stop talking**:

> *"Before a critical deal moment, how do you decide what to do next?"*

Their answer reads as one of three:

| Answer shape | Read |
|---|---|
| No clear answer / *"good question"* / pauses | **Strong candidate.** They feel the gap; they don't have a process. |
| Vague or reactive process (*"I just talk to the AE"* / *"we run a deal review"*) | **Strong candidate.** Process exists but lives in heads, not state. |
| Structured, confident answer (named gates, owners, timing) | **Weaker fit (for now).** They've solved the surface problem; selling them is harder until something specific breaks their process. |

Whatever they say, **don't pivot to selling.** The next move is to ask them to walk you through how they applied that process to a specific recent deal — that's where the real gaps surface.

If their answer is structured but generic ("we use MEDDPICC"), that's *also* a strong candidate — they have the rubric, but probably not the operational layer that enforces it under pressure. Ask: *"How do you check the rubric is actually being applied on a deal you can't afford to lose this week?"*

## What landing sounds like

Quotes that = real signal. Each one contains at least two of: **surprise**, **action**, **attribution**.

- *"This helped me decide what to do before the call."*
- *"This showed me what was actually going to break."*
- *"I called [name] because of this — I wouldn't have without it."*
- *"I would've gone in differently without this."*
- *"My VP needs to see this."*
- *"When can I see this on a real account on my list?"*
- *"This is the gap — I've been trying to describe it and you just did."*
- *"How quickly can you get me access?"*
- *"Can I forward this to [specific name]?"*

Any of these unprompted = the framing landed. Move forward.

## What polite interest sounds like

Quotes that look like signal but aren't. These are what people say when they like you and don't want to be rude. None of them contain action or attribution; they're surprise (real or performed) without commitment.

- *"Interesting."*
- *"Looks clean."*
- *"Cool."*
- *"Good thinking."*
- *"This is needed in the market."*
- *"Send me a follow-up."*
- *"Let me think about it."*
- *"Have you talked to [name at another company]?"* (deflection — they're outsourcing the decision)
- *"We have something kind of like this internally already."* (also a no, with face-saving)
- *"Useful framing."*

Any of these = treat as a no. Don't follow up beyond a single bump. Move on.

## The diagnostic shape

When the call ends, ask yourself three questions:

1. **Did they get specific?** Did they reference a real deal, a real stakeholder, a real moment? Generic = polite. Specific = real.
2. **Did they tell you what they'd do with it?** Even if it's *"send to my VP,"* that's an action. Without an action, the surprise is theatrical.
3. **Did they ask a follow-up question?** Real interest produces *"how does it know X?"* / *"what would it have caught on Y?"* — they're stress-testing it. Polite interest produces no follow-ups, just affirmations.

If you got 2 or 3 of those, it's signal. If you got 0 or 1, file it as polite and don't over-read.

### Hard rule: behavior or specificity, never tone

Tone is the loudest variable in the room and the least predictive. Excited delivery, a laugh, lots of *"yeah totally"* — none of it counts. The filter is binary:

- **Did they say something specific to their world?** (Names a deal, a stakeholder, a recent moment.) → Count.
- **Did they describe a behavior they'd take or did take?** ("I'd call X." / "I tried that with Y.") → Count.
- **Did they only express enthusiasm without anchoring it to anything concrete?** → Discount, regardless of how warm it sounded.

Examples to ground the rule:

| What they said | Read | Why |
|---|---|---|
| *"This is interesting."* | Discount | No specificity, no behavior — just tone. |
| *"Looks really clean."* | Discount | Tone, no anchor. |
| *"I'd call [name] before the meeting."* | **Count.** | Specific person + specific action. |
| *"Can you do this on my pipeline?"* | **Strong signal.** | Names the asset, requests behavior. |
| *"My VP needs to see this."* | **Strong signal.** | Action + named owner. |
| *"This would've caught the [Acme] thing last quarter."* | **Strong signal.** | Specific past deal + counterfactual reasoning. |
| *"Useful framing, definitely something there."* | Discount | Generic tone, no specificity. |
| *"Send me a calendar link."* | Half-count | Behavior, no specificity. Wait for what happens at the meeting. |

## What to do when it lands (3/3 signal)

When you get the strong-signal version, the temptation is to:
- Pitch harder
- Show more
- Schedule a demo
- Send a deck

**Don't.** Every one of those resets the asymmetry you just earned. The right move is exactly one sentence:

> *"Give me one deal and read-only access — I'll run this against it and show you tomorrow."*

That sentence is doing four things at once:

| Component | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **"One deal"** | Bounded scope — they're not committing to a pilot, just a target. |
| **"Read-only access"** | Lowest possible permission ask. No write-back, no integration, no risk. |
| **"I'll run this against it"** | You're doing the work, not them. They don't have to clean data or schedule anything. |
| **"And show you tomorrow"** | Time-boxed urgency. Not "next week" — tomorrow. Forces a near-term return moment that makes the asymmetry concrete. |

This is the **credibility primitive** in execution form — show value before access. They give you a company name + read-only. You spend an hour pulling SEC + earnings + news + LinkedIn signals + whatever's in their substrate. You produce 1 insight (per the Intelligence-demo recipe) that ties an external signal to a deal-motion implication. You send it back inside 24 hours.

If the insight earns *"how did you see that?"* — they'll bring the next conversation. You haven't asked for anything yet, and you've created the asymmetry that makes a paid relationship the rep's idea, not yours.

**Do not:**
- Schedule a demo before the read-only is granted
- Send a deck "to give them context"
- Offer a free trial of the product (this is a different motion — credibility now, product later)
- Promise more than one insight on the first round (you want them asking *"can you do this on more of my pipeline?"* — not waiting for you to deliver something exhaustive)

## What to do when it doesn't land

When you get the polite-interest version, the temptation is to:
- Re-pitch
- Send more context
- Try a different angle
- Convince them they're wrong

Don't. The right move is the smallest possible exit:

- *"Got it — really appreciate the time. If something changes on your end, I know where to find you."*

Then log what didn't land in `design-partner-shortlist.md`, and move to the next conversation.

## After 3 conversations

The pitch and the discovery question will refine themselves *if* you log honestly after each one. Two questions to answer, in writing, after every call:

1. **What phrase or framing produced the strongest reaction (positive or negative)?** Use it again, or drop it.
2. **What did I assume they'd respond to that they didn't?** That's the gap between your model of the buyer and the actual buyer.

After 3 calls, the pattern in your log is the answer. Don't change the pitch on a single call's reaction — wait for the n=3 pattern.

## The log — three fields, no narrative

Per interaction, capture exactly three things and nothing more:

| Field | What goes here |
|---|---|
| **What they said** | One or two lines, verbatim. Their words, not your paraphrase. If they didn't reply, write "no response." |
| **What they did** | The behavior. Took the meeting / asked a follow-up question / forwarded to their VP / went silent. Action or no action. |
| **Classification** | 0/3 to 3/3 from the diagnostic above. Single number. |

That's it. No narrative. No *"I think they might be interested because…"* — that's story, and story corrupts the data.

Why the constraint matters:

- **Forces separation of data from interpretation.** The temptation after a warm call is to write a paragraph rationalizing why it went well. The three fields make that impossible — you can only record what they said and what they did.
- **Lets you compare across conversations without bias.** Five rows of three fields each is scannable. Five paragraphs of warm interpretation is not.
- **Becomes your first real dataset for patterning later.** When you eventually have 30 conversations to learn from, you want clean signal — verbatim quotes, observed behavior, consistent classification. Not a narrative archive.

## The week's stop condition (pre-commit)

Decide *before* any reply lands what each shape of week-end result triggers. Pre-commitment is the only way to avoid letting a single hot quote pull you forward or a single cold one pull you back.

| Week-end result | What it triggers |
|---|---|
| **One clear 3/3 signal** | Proceed to next step — say *"give me one deal and read-only access — I'll show you tomorrow,"* and execute the Intelligence-demo recipe. Don't expand outreach to more people; the 3/3 is the lead, not a sample. |
| **2–3 consistent 0/3 or weak signals** | Pause and re-examine the framing. Don't reach out to a fourth person yet. The pitch isn't landing; sending it again won't change that. Re-read what they actually said (the verbatim log) and look for the phrase that didn't fit their world. |
| **Mixed / ambiguous** | Run one more rep — same role, different person — *and* one more deal context. n=2 isn't enough; n=3 starts to show pattern. Don't decide off mixed. |

This prevents two opposite mistakes:

- **Overreacting to one strong hit.** A single 3/3 from a friendly contact in your network can be a relationship signal, not a market signal. Treat it as a lead, not a verdict.
- **Dismissing too early.** Two flat reads from people who didn't fit the rubric isn't proof the framing is wrong — it's proof your shortlist needs tightening.

## Decision latency: act within 24 hours of a 3/3

When the first real signal comes in, the risk isn't missing it. The risk is sitting on it too long while you "think about it" or "line up a couple more calls first."

Pre-commit:

> **3/3 signal → act within 24 hours.**

That means: same day or next day, you say *"give me one deal and read-only access — I'll show you tomorrow,"* and you start the Intelligence-demo work. Not *"let me get back to you next week."* Not *"let me run a few more calls and pick the best one."*

Speed is part of the signal. The 24-hour window is what makes the asymmetry feel real to them — proof that you weren't fishing, you were waiting for them. Latency past that window converts a strong moment into a normal vendor cadence, and the asymmetry collapses.

If you can't move within 24 hours (travel, capacity, conflicting priority), don't get the signal. Pull the outreach the week before until you can.

## Don't dilute the first win

When the first 3/3 hits, the instinct will be:

- Add features to the demo
- Broaden the scope ("let me show you across three deals")
- Bring in more stakeholders ("can your VP join?")
- Polish the story for the second meeting

**Don't.** All of those expand the wedge before you've confirmed it wasn't a single-relationship anomaly.

The right move after a first 3/3 is to **replicate the exact same motion on a second deal**, same framing, same constraint, same scope:

- Same one-sentence problem statement
- Same single discovery anchor question
- Same "give me one deal and read-only access — I'll show you tomorrow"
- Same Intelligence-demo recipe (1 prospect, 1–2 insights max, X→Y→Z grammar)

You're not scaling yet. You're confirming the first win wasn't luck. n=2 with the same motion is what earns the right to start varying anything.

If the second hit also lands at 3/3, *then* you can think about expanding. Until then: replicate.

## Expect the first signal to feel smaller than you want

When the first real validation comes, it won't sound like *"this changes everything."* It will sound more like:

> *"yeah… I actually did that before the call."*

Or:

> *"this caught the thing I missed."*

Or:

> *"send this to my VP."*

Quiet, almost dismissive. Real validation usually understates itself because the rep is *already moving on to the next thing they need to do.* If they got dramatic, they'd be performing for you instead of acting on it.

Don't wait for the louder version. The quiet one IS the signal. Re-read the diagnostic shape — specific + behavior + follow-up question — and trust the framework over your need for the moment to feel bigger.

## Silent signals count too

Not every signal is verbal. The leaning-in person may say very little explicitly and instead:

- Send you a deal name without being asked
- Reference your framing unprompted in a later message ("the *before-it-happens* thing you mentioned…")
- Come back with *"can you look at this one too?"* before you've even finished the first
- Forward your message to a colleague and CC you
- Add you on a quick mid-week call that wasn't on the calendar

These behaviors **count as 3/3 signal**, even without an explicit verbal quote. Behavior is the harder evidence; quotes are the supporting evidence. Don't wait for someone to say something quotable when they've already done something quotable.

Update the log accordingly: under "what they did," capture these silent moves with the same weight as explicit quotes under "what they said."

## Protecting the "tomorrow" promise

When you say *"I'll show you tomorrow,"* that promise IS the credibility multiplier. If it slips, you don't lose neutral ground — you lose the asymmetry you just created.

Rules:

- **Only say it if you can actually deliver.** No conditional "tomorrow." If you've got a busy day, say *"by Friday"* and mean it.
- **Tomorrow's deliverable must be fast, concrete, and specific to their deal.** Not a generic doc. Not a screenshot of someone else's data. The Intelligence-demo recipe (1 prospect → SEC + earnings + news → 1 insight that ties external signal to deal-motion implication) IS the deliverable shape. If you can't hit that bar, push the date.
- **If you can't deliver as promised, send a one-line acknowledgment ahead of the deadline.** Better to say *"this is taking longer than I want — by EOD Thursday"* before tomorrow ends than to silently slip. Silence after a "tomorrow" promise is the worst possible read.

The asymmetry isn't fragile because of one missed delivery — it's fragile because of *unacknowledged* missed delivery. Buy time before the deadline, not after.

## Scope discipline when interest shows up

When the framing lands, the leaning-in person will try to expand the scope. They'll ask:

- *"Can this integrate with our [Salesforce / Gong / Outreach / pipeline tool]?"*
- *"What about our whole pipeline?"*
- *"Could you do this for the whole sales team?"*
- *"What about post-close — implementation tracking?"*

These feel like buying signals. They're not — they're *scope expansion that kills the wedge*. The right response is always the same:

> *"Eventually yes, but right now I want to nail one deal, one decision moment, one action. Pick the deal that matters most this week and we'll start there."*

Pull every conversation back to: **one deal, one decision moment, one action.** That's the entire wedge. Anything wider dilutes the demo, dilutes the read, and turns a sharp moment into a feature comparison.

Scope expansion is the moment to enforce constraints, not relax them. The wedge earned the conversation; expanding the wedge to win the conversation loses both.

## Let the messy reads stay messy

The biggest risk in the calibration framework is the *opposite* of over-interpreting politeness — it's force-fitting partial, inconsistent, or ambiguous feedback into a clean category too early.

Real reads are usually messy:

- They sound interested in the call but don't reply for a week.
- They ask one specific question (count) and give two pieces of generic praise (discount).
- They say *"this is the gap"* and then never name the deal you asked about.
- They're hot on the framing and lukewarm on the proof of value.

**Don't force a verdict from a single conversation.** The 0/3 → 3/3 diagnostic is a per-call tool, not a per-relationship one. Across three calls, the pattern stabilizes. Within one call, ambiguity is the default.

If a single call comes back ambiguous:
- Log it as ambiguous. Don't promote it to "real signal" because you want it to be.
- Don't demote it to "polite no" because the strong-signal sentence didn't land.
- Wait for n=2 or n=3 before deciding whether the framing is the problem or the buyer is.

Single-call certainty is almost always an over-read in either direction.

## The validation phrase you're listening for

Beneath everything: one sentence, in their words, that you'd put on a slide if you were pitching this to investors next quarter. Something that contains the surprise + action + attribution triad in their voice, not yours.

You'll know it when you hear it. Until then, you're learning, not validating.
