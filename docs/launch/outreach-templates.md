# Outreach copy templates — first conversations

*Goal: get 30 minutes on the calendar to test whether the framing lands. Not to pitch. Not to recruit "design partners" — that's the wrong opener and signals what you're after too early.*

## The frame

Every message has the same shape:

1. **Acknowledge the relationship** (we've worked together / we last spoke at X) — short, one line.
2. **Lead with the problem, not the product.** State the specific gap you've been working on. Don't name a system, don't say "design partner," don't pitch a category.
3. **One specific question that's flattering to be asked** (their experience, not your product).
4. **Easy out** if it's not the moment.

Length cap: **5 sentences.** Anything longer is a deck.

---

## Template A — Warm tie, currently a CRO / VP Sales

**Subject:** Quick brain-pick on a deal-execution question

> Hey [first name],
>
> I've been working on something that surfaces the 1–2 actions most likely to cause a deal to fail before it happens. Curious how you currently make that call before a big meeting — when one of your biggest deals is two weeks from close, how do you decide what your AE should do *this week* to not lose it?
>
> 30 min, no slides, no demo. If you've solved this cleanly internally, the call is short and I learn something. If it's a question you wrestle with, I'd love to hear how you currently think about it.
>
> Open to next Tuesday or Thursday afternoon?
>
> — Brendan

**Why it works:** Leads with the problem in one sentence (loss-framed, time-anchored, action-oriented). No "AI for sales" or "design partner" framing — both signal vendor mode and trigger filtering. The "if you've solved it cleanly the call is short" line is permission to say so without losing face — which means the ones who *don't* have it solved are signaled by them taking the meeting.

## Template C — Slightly colder tie (worked together but ≥2 years ago)

**Subject:** Long shot — quick question

> [First name] — long shot ask. We worked together at [shared context, brief]. I've been quietly building something in the sales-execution space and I'm trying to validate it against people who currently sit in CRO / VP Sales seats at mid-market companies — your shoes today.
>
> One question: when a deal you can't afford to lose has two weeks left, what's actually broken about how you and your AE figure out the next move?
>
> 30 min, fully a research conversation, no pitch on the call. If it's not the moment, no worries — would just appreciate the time to listen if it is.
>
> — Brendan

**Why it works:** The "long shot" opener gives you permission to be slightly forward despite the gap in contact. The question is sharper ("what's actually broken") because they don't owe you politeness on the answer.

## Template D — Reply to a specific recent post / signal

**Subject:** Saw your [post/comment/talk] on [topic]

> [First name] — saw your [thing] on [topic] last week. Your line about [specific thing they said] is exactly the gap I'm trying to address.
>
> I'm building a system for the moment between *"we know this deal could slip"* and *"we know what to do this week to stop it from slipping."* Most CROs I've talked to are still running that mostly out of their AE's head.
>
> Would 30 min make sense? Research call, no pitch — would love your read on whether I'm even thinking about the right gap.
>
> — Brendan

**Why it works:** Specificity earns the meeting. Generic "I saw your post" lines are filtered; quoting the actual line they wrote isn't. Use this when there's a real signal to anchor on.

## What NOT to send

- *"Want to be a design partner?"* — signals you're recruiting before they've decided whether the framing even fits. Wait until they're leaning in to use that word.
- *"Want to try our AI sales tool?"* — kills the discovery frame instantly. They tune out before reading.
- *"Quick demo?"* — same problem. You're now one of fifty cold outbounds this week.
- *"We help reps win more deals."* — every vendor says this. They're filtering for something specific, not a category-fit pitch.
- Any subject line with "AI", "transform", or "platform" — filtered before opening. *"RevOps"* is fine if you have a credible RevOps thread already; *"sales-execution"* reads less generic than *"sales."*

## What to do if they say yes

1. Confirm a 30-minute slot, not an hour. (Hour-long invites get rescheduled; 30-min calls happen.)
2. Send no agenda, no doc, no link in advance. The call is the discovery question, not a presentation.
3. Have **one** target company on your list ready in case they ask *"can you show me what you mean?"* — that's where the Intelligence-demo recipe activates. Don't open with it; only deploy if they earn it by leaning in.

## One iteration per contact

This is the discipline that protects signal purity. Per person:

> **one outreach → one follow-up → one next move if it lands**

No multi-threading. No parallel pitches. No *"let me try one more angle."* If the sequence doesn't produce a 3/3 read, archive the contact and move on.

Why this matters:

- **Preserves signal purity.** Running three angles past one person turns the read into a referendum on your messaging tactics, not on whether the framing fits their world.
- **Prevents manufactured engagement.** If you push hard enough, almost anyone in your network will give you a polite call. That call is worth nothing.
- **Keeps the read attributable to a single frame.** When you refine the pitch later, you want to know which version of the message produced which reaction. One iteration per person = one clean data point.

If it doesn't land after the sequence: archive, log the result (see `calibration.md` log fields), don't reach out again until you have a meaningfully different reason to.

## Cadence + same-day follow-up rules

The bigger trap than no response is a *partial* response that you let stall. Each response shape gets one specific move:

### They reply but don't engage deeply

**Within an hour**, send one concrete hook tied to their reality, not yours:

> *"Happy to show you how this would apply to one of your current deals — pick one and I'll map it."*

Why this works: it converts the soft yes into a specific action. They name a deal → you have something to demo against. They don't → the politeness was the answer.

### No response in 24 hours (warm contact)

**One nudge max,** then stop:

> *"Worth a quick look before your next big deal review — can show it in 10 mins."*

Why this works: time-anchors to their next forecast call. Lowers the cost of yes (10 min, not 30).

### No response after the nudge

**Drop.** Don't chase. The signal is already weak; chasing makes it weaker for next time.

### Standard cold-cadence rules

- **No follow-up emails** until 7 days after the *initial* send (separate from the same-day rule above, which is in response to a partial reply).
- **One follow-up max**, single line: *"Bumping this up — happy to take a no, just don't want it lost."*
- **Move on** after that. Network is finite; preserve relationship integrity for the next time you reach out.
